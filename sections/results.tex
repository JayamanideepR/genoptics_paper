\section{Results}

This section evaluates whether generated tabular lens rows can be converted into usable optical prescriptions. The results address two questions. First, we benchmark generative models under the final Patent+Synth-full, tuned-representation, physics-based post-processing pipeline. Second, we isolate the contribution of the main pipeline components introduced in this work. Section~\ref{sec:results_synthetic_expansion} compares Patent-only and Patent+Synth-full training across the evaluated generators and reports full-data statistical diagnostics. Section~\ref{sec:results_tuned_representation} isolates the optics-aware tuned representation, Section~\ref{sec:results_dataset_scale} studies synthetic dataset scale for TabSyn, and Section~\ref{sec:results_raytrace_postproc} isolates physics-based post-processing.

Unless otherwise stated, we use the following defaults:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Each run samples 10{,}000 prescriptions from a fixed trained-model configuration.
    \item Sampling is repeated across four sampling seeds, and reported values are mean \ensuremath{\pm} sample standard deviation across seeds. These are sampling seeds from fixed trained-model configurations, not independent retraining seeds.
    \item Patent+Synth-full training, tuned parameterization, and ray-tracing-based post-processing are used unless a subsection specifies a controlled comparison.
\end{enumerate}

Our primary outcome is the simulation-verified feasible sample fraction: the percentage of generated samples that pass post-processing and CodeV preparation and then return from CodeV without an error. This denominator includes both generated prescriptions rejected before simulation and prescriptions that reach CodeV but return an error. The evaluation stages and metric families follow the benchmark protocol in Section~\ref{sec:benchmark_models_evaluation}.

Figure~\ref{fig:results_success_dashboard} gives a compact overview of the main feasible-yield comparisons and the full-data statistical diagnostics.

\subsection{Synthetic-Expanded Training Data Improves Feasible Generation}
\label{sec:results_synthetic_expansion}

We first test whether synthetic-expanded training changes the downstream population of usable optical prescriptions, rather than only increasing the amount of tabular data available to the generator. The comparison is intentionally cross-model: a useful expansion should improve feasible generation across more than one generative architecture.

Table~\ref{tab:section_6_1_synthetic_expansion_summary} shows that synthetic-expanded training substantially increases the end-to-end feasible fraction for all evaluated generators. CoDi, STaSY, and TabSyn all gain more than 49 percentage points, while the already strong SMOTE-style reference gains an additional 11.45 percentage points.

Feasible yield is the primary optics-facing metric, but it does not by itself characterize distributional fidelity, local redundancy, or train proximity. Table~\ref{tab:section_6_1_statistical_diversity_novelty} therefore reports full-data statistical, diversity, and novelty diagnostics. These diagnostics are computed in the tuned representation before optical filtering, within active surface-count groups. Surface-count TVD measures topology-frequency mismatch. Conditional marginal discrepancy averages active-column single-column discrepancies within surface-count groups and weights groups by reference-row support. Intra-generated nearest-neighbor distance measures local redundancy among generated rows, and DCR excess measures train proximity after subtracting the expected train-closer fraction implied by the train/test support ratio.

The statistical diagnostics clarify the benchmark trade-off. The SMOTE-style reference best matches the training distribution and gives the highest feasible yield, but it also has the lowest intra-generated nearest-neighbor distance and the largest DCR excess, consistent with local interpolation. TabSyn provides the strongest learned-model compromise in the full pipeline: it is close to SMOTE on surface-count fidelity, has substantially higher local diversity than SMOTE, and has DCR excess comparable to CoDi and STaSY. STaSY produces the highest intra-generated distance and slightly lower DCR excess, but with weaker distribution fidelity and lower feasible yield. CoDi generates many feasible rows but is less well matched to the reference distribution.

\subsection{Effect of Tuned Optical Representation}
\label{sec:results_tuned_representation}

Having established the benefit of synthetic-expanded training, we next isolate the representation used to expose optical prescriptions to the generator. The tuned representation is designed to make generated rows easier to reconstruct into physically meaningful lens prescriptions; the relevant test is therefore whether it improves final feasibility under the same downstream evaluation.

Table~\ref{tab:tuned_untuned_results} shows that the tuned representation substantially improves feasible generation. The feasible sample fraction increases from 49.51\% to 83.72\%, a 34.21 percentage-point gain. This improvement comes from reducing both material/geometric infeasibility and ray-tracing infeasibility.

\subsection{Effect of Synthetic Dataset Scale}
\label{sec:results_dataset_scale}

We then examine whether the synthetic-expanded dataset continues to help as its size increases. This comparison uses TabSyn because it is the strongest learned generator in the full pipeline and because its diffusion training was retuned for the larger dataset sizes.

The feasible fraction improves monotonically across the evaluated dataset sizes: 18.62\% for Patent-only, 74.20\% for Patent+Synth-250k, 80.25\% for Patent+Synth-500k, and 83.72\% for Patent+Synth-full. Relative to Patent+Synth-250k, Patent+Synth-full adds 9.51 percentage points of feasible yield. This scaling trend indicates that the synthetic-expanded dataset remains useful beyond the 250k setting when the diffusion learning rate is retuned for the larger training sets.

\subsection{Effect of Physics-Based Post-Processing}
\label{sec:results_raytrace_postproc}

The final ablation separates learned generation from deterministic physics-based post-processing. This post-processing step does not train a new generator; instead, it attempts to repair reconstructed prescriptions before CodeV preparation. Its contribution is therefore measured by how many additional generated rows become simulation-ready and successful.

Physics-based post-processing increases the end-to-end feasible yield from 67.29\% to 83.72\%, a gain of 16.43 percentage points (Table~\ref{tab:raytrace_postproc_benefit}). The main effect is a reduction in material/geometric infeasibility from 22.69\% to 1.55\%. Ray-tracing infeasibility increases from 10.02\% to 14.73\%, but the net infeasible fraction still decreases from 32.70\% to 16.28\%.

Feasibility alone does not show whether the correction degrades optical quality. Figure~\ref{fig:results_postproc_metric_reduction_heatmap_full} therefore compares paired optical metrics on common rows that were submitted in both branches and returned successfully in both CodeV runs. Each cell reports the per-seed median paired percent reduction after post-processing, averaged across the four sampling seeds, for a metric-field combination. Positive values indicate lower metric values after post-processing. Spot-size and encircled-energy metrics are normalized by the corresponding Airy diameter before comparison, while the nominal performance metric is left in its returned units. This paired comparison is a within-sample post-processing diagnostic rather than a ranking of unrelated generated designs.
