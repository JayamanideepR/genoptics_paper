\section{Results}

This section evaluates how generated tabular lens rows become usable optical prescriptions. Unless otherwise stated, each run starts from 10,000 generated samples, and repeated-run values are reported as mean \ensuremath{\pm} sample standard deviation across four sampling seeds. These are sampling seeds from fixed trained-model configurations, not independent retraining seeds. We report the \emph{simulation-verified feasible sample fraction}: the percentage of generated samples that pass post-processing and CodeV/LLESP preparation and then return from CodeV without an error. This denominator is stricter than a success rate on submitted samples because it also counts samples rejected before simulation.

\subsection{Synthetic-Expanded Training Data Improves Feasible Generation}
\label{sec:results_synthetic_expansion}

The first experiment asks whether the synthetic-expanded training set improves usable generation under the final pipeline. To isolate this effect, the representation is fixed to the tuned parameterization, NRTE post-processing is enabled, and each model is compared between patent-only training and the patent-plus-synthetic 250k training variant. Model hyperparameters are held fixed within each model family; the intended variable in this subsection is the training data source, not model size, representation, or post-processing.

Table~\ref{tab:section_6_1_synthetic_expansion_summary} shows the feasible fraction for the matched model families. The synthetic-expanded 250k dataset substantially increases the end-to-end feasible fraction for the learned tabular generators that can use the added data. TabSyn increases from 26.57\% feasible samples under patent-only training to 84.61\% under patent-plus-synthetic 250k training, STaSy increases from 0.54\% to 61.84\%, and CoDi increases from 7.09\% to 56.96\%. The SMOTE-style reference is already highly feasible on patent-only training, but still increases from 86.84\% to 98.16\%. TabDDPM remains near zero under patent-plus-synthetic training, showing that the benefit of synthetic-expanded training is not automatic across all tabular generators.

Feasible yield is the primary optics-facing metric, but it does not by itself show whether the generated rows match the model-facing training distribution. Table~\ref{tab:section_6_1_all_sample_diagnostics} therefore combines the 250k feasible fraction with all-row statistical and novelty diagnostics computed directly in the tuned representation before optical reconstruction, NRTE, and CodeV/LLESP filtering. Surface-count total variation distance measures topology-frequency mismatch. Numeric marginal KS, \texttt{isair} marginal error, and pairwise dependency error are computed within active surface-count groups and then aggregated with training-reference row weights. DCR excess measures same-surface-count train proximity after correcting for the train/test support ratio.

SMOTE is included here as an interpolation reference rather than as a learned generative model. Its high feasible fraction and low distribution errors are expected because samples are constructed locally from existing prescriptions, and its DCR excess is the largest, consistent with strong train proximity. Among the learned generators, TabSyn gives the best operating point: it has the highest feasible fraction and the lowest surface-count, numeric, \texttt{isair}, and pairwise errors among the learned models. STaSy produces a sizable feasible set but has larger surface-count and active-parameter mismatch. CoDi is now included in the benchmark and produces many feasible rows, but its distribution diagnostics are weaker than TabSyn's under this setup. TabDDPM shows why benchmark adaptation matters: its active numeric, \texttt{isair}, and pairwise errors are large and its feasible fraction remains near zero.

Together, Tables~\ref{tab:section_6_1_synthetic_expansion_summary} and~\ref{tab:section_6_1_all_sample_diagnostics} support two conclusions. First, synthetic-expanded training strongly improves feasible generation for TabSyn, STaSy, and CoDi, while TabDDPM remains a failure case under the same benchmark pipeline. Second, feasibility must still be measured after optical reconstruction and simulation-readiness checks. A generator can produce rows that look tabularly plausible while still failing material reconstruction, geometric consistency checks, or CodeV execution. The mutually exclusive failure-mode breakdown in Appendix~\ref{app:section_6_1_failure_breakdown} shows that the 250k runs reduce the large failure mass seen in patent-only learned-model runs, but the remaining failures are now dominated primarily by CodeV errors for the successful 250k branches.

\subsection{Effect Of Tuned Optical Representation}

The second experiment isolates the effect of the model-facing representation. The dataset is fixed to patent-plus-synthetic 250k, NRTE post-processing is enabled, and the comparison is restricted to model families with matched tuned and untuned returned runs. This keeps the intended variable as the optical representation rather than dataset size or post-processing.

Table~\ref{tab:tuned_untuned_results} shows that the tuned representation has little effect on the SMOTE-style reference but a large effect on TabSyn. For SMOTE, both representations submit nearly all generated rows to CodeV/LLESP and return nearly all submitted rows successfully, giving no meaningful feasible-yield change. For TabSyn, the tuned branch improves both CodeV-input acceptance and success among submitted rows: acceptance rises from 84.66\% to 98.69\%, and success among submitted rows rises from 55.92\% to 85.73\%. The resulting simulation-verified feasible fraction increases from 47.34\% to 84.61\%, a 37.27 percentage-point gain. This indicates that the tuned representation improves both pre-simulation prescription validity and downstream CodeV robustness.

\subsection{Effect Of Synthetic Dataset Scale}

To isolate dataset scale from the representation ablation, Table~\ref{tab:dataset_scale_results} fixes the generator to TabSyn, the parameterization to the tuned representation, and the post-processing branch to NRTE. Since the paper-level question is how dataset scale changes the final usable sample population, the table reports the end-to-end feasible yield and a mutually exclusive breakdown of infeasible rows rather than intermediate pipeline gates.

The patent-only row is retained only as the low-data anchor. The main dataset effect is the transition from patent-only to patent-plus-synthetic training, where the feasible yield rises from 26.57\% to 84.61\%. Beyond 250k synthetic-expanded training rows, this fixed TabSyn configuration does not improve monotonically: the 500k and full-dataset runs remain high, but are slightly below the 250k run. The additional infeasibility in the larger variants is primarily CodeV error rather than material or geometry rejection. Thus, the million-scale dataset is best interpreted as a released scaling endpoint and resource for higher-capacity or retuned models, not as a guarantee that the same off-the-shelf TabSyn configuration will be optimal at the largest scale.

\subsection{Physics Based Post-Processing Benefit}
\label{sec:results_raytrace_postproc}

The final ablation evaluates the optional ray-tracing based post-processing layer. The generator, representation, and training data are fixed to TabSyn with the tuned representation on the patent-plus-synthetic 250k training variant. The only intended variable is whether generated prescriptions pass directly to CodeV/LLESP preparation or first pass through NRTE.

NRTE increases the end-to-end feasible yield from 67.41\% to 84.61\%, a gain of 17.20 percentage points under the same 10{,}000-sample denominator (Table~\ref{tab:raytrace_postproc_benefit}). The success rate among rows submitted to CodeV/LLESP is nearly unchanged, so the gain comes primarily from moving more generated prescriptions into the simulation-ready set rather than from changing the downstream solver success rate.

Feasibility alone does not show whether the correction damages optical quality. Figure~\ref{fig:results_nrte_metric_reduction_heatmap_250k} therefore compares paired optical metrics on common rows that were submitted in both branches and returned successfully in both CodeV runs. Each cell reports the per-seed median paired percent reduction after NRTE, averaged across the four sampling seeds, for a metric-field combination. Positive values indicate lower metric values after NRTE. Spot-size and encircled-energy metrics are normalized by the corresponding Airy diameter before comparison, while the nominal performance metric is left in its returned units. This paired comparison is a within-sample post-processing diagnostic rather than a ranking of unrelated generated designs.
