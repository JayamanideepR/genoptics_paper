\section{Proposed Conditional Generative Model}

The proposed model is designed to address structure that is weakly expressed in fixed-width tabular representations. Optical prescriptions are ordered assemblies of surfaces: the same curvature, thickness, or material value has different meaning depending on its surface index, and designs can contain different numbers of active surfaces. A model operating directly on flattened columns must infer this ordering and masking structure indirectly. We therefore represent each prescription as an ordered sequence of optics-aware tokens and learn a latent generative model over this sequence representation.

The framework has three components: a composite tokenizer that maps surface-parameter values into tokens, a transformer autoencoder that compresses and reconstructs prescriptions, and a conditional latent diffusion model that samples the learned latent space. \subsection{Composite Tokenizer}

For compatibility with transformer-based architectures, each optical prescription is converted into a sequence of parameter tokens. Each token represents a tuple of surface index, parameter type, and parameter value. The surface index tells the model where the parameter belongs in the optical assembly, the parameter type distinguishes curvature from thickness, material, aperture, and other quantities, and the parameter value carries the numerical or categorical content.

The tokenizer uses the following embeddings:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Value embedding.} Numerical parameters are embedded using learnable affine projections. Categorical parameters are embedded using lookup tables.
  \item \textbf{Parameter-type embedding.} A learnable type embedding is added so that the model can distinguish different optical quantities even when their numerical ranges overlap.
  \item \textbf{Surface-position embedding.} Surface ordering along the optical axis is encoded using relative Fourier features. For the $i^{th}$ surface in a system with $N$ active surfaces, a normalized position $\frac{i}{N-1}$ is mapped to sinusoidal Fourier features and then projected to the model dimension.
  \item \textbf{Validity mask.} Tokens belonging to inactive padded surfaces are masked so that they do not contribute to attention pooling or reconstruction loss.
\end{enumerate}

The aperture stop is treated separately from ordinary surface parameters. Rather than inserting the stop index as a surface token, the decoder predicts it using a dedicated stop-index head from the latent representation.

\subsection{Transformer-Based Autoencoder}
The encoder-decoder architecture adapts the transformer formulation introduced in \cite{vaswani2023attentionneed}, with the optics-specific architecture summarized in Fig.~\ref{fig:ar_latent_diffusion_architecture}. The encoder operates on the tokens generated by the composite tokenizer, capturing both intra-surface parameter interactions and inter-surface relationships along the optical axis. Rather than passing all encoder outputs directly to the generative model, we aggregate them into a compact fixed-dimensional latent representation using learned attention pooling.

The decoder is implemented as an autoregressive transformer that receives the latent representation through cross-attention layers and reconstructs individual parameters in surface-major, parameter-minor order. Causal masking prevents the decoder from seeing future output tokens during training. Encoder-side and decoder-side token embeddings are not shared, allowing the encoder to represent observed prescriptions and the decoder to represent partially reconstructed output sequences with separate parameters.

The autoencoder is trained with teacher forcing on the decoder side. Numerical parameters use reconstruction losses, categorical parameters use classification losses, and inactive-surface positions are masked out of the loss. The aperture stop head is trained with a separate stop-index loss. After training, the encoder maps each training prescription into the fixed-dimensional latent space used by the diffusion model.

\subsection{Conditional Latent Diffusion Model}

Latent representations produced by the transformer autoencoder are flattened and paired with their corresponding design conditions to train a conditional diffusion model. In the planned conditional experiments, the conditioning vector contains target design specifications such as effective focal length, entrance pupil diameter, and maximum field angle \textcolor{red}{[final condition set to be confirmed]}. We adopt the diffusion framework provided by the smalldiffusion package \cite{Yuan2025_smalldiffusion}, using its standard noise scheduling, training objective, and sampling procedure. Modifications are introduced in the conditioning mechanism and denoising network to support multiple continuous optical design conditions.

To condition the denoising model, we use feature-wise linear modulation (FiLM) \cite{perez2017filmvisualreasoninggeneral}. Condition values are embedded using a small neural network and then passed to FiLM layers that modulate intermediate denoising features. This gives the diffusion prior a direct mechanism for shifting the sampled latent distribution according to requested optical specifications.

The conditional diffusion model is trained with the standard classifier-free guidance formulation. Training minimizes a mean squared error loss between predicted and true noise over the predefined noise schedule, with conditioning inputs randomly dropped and replaced by a null condition. During inference, guided sampling combines conditional and unconditional predictions using a guidance scale. Generated latent samples are reshaped back to the autoencoder latent format, decoded autoregressively into optical parameters, and passed through the post-processing pipeline outlined in Fig.~\ref{fig:model_training_pipeline}.
