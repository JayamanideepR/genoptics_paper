\section{Dataset}

We construct a large-scale optical prescription dataset for generative modeling by combining patent-derived lens designs with synthetic designs generated through controlled perturbation and post-processing of those patents. Each design is retained in two linked forms: an individual CodeV sequence file for optical simulation and a common tabular prescription row for data analysis and downstream model training. The tabular representation stores the design parameters, design conditions, material assignments, optical-performance annotations, and provenance fields needed to connect each row back to its patent source design. The patent-derived designs provide realistic reference families across surface counts, optical configurations, apertures, field angles, and material choices, while the synthetic expansion increases coverage around those families in the same tabular prescription format. The remainder of this section further expands on the details of the dataset in the following order: Section~\ref{sec:dataset_patent_reference} introduces the patent-derived reference corpus and its optical prescription representation. Section~\ref{sec:dataset_patent_shortlisting} describes the standardization and shortlisting procedure, and Section~\ref{sec:dataset_synthetic_expansion} presents the traceable synthetic expansion workflow and resulting dataset.

\subsection{Patent-Derived Reference Designs}
\label{sec:dataset_patent_reference}

The collected patent-derived reference corpus comprises 2,571 optical system designs assembled from three sources: the CodeV patent library~\cite{codev}, which primarily consists of Japanese and Russian patents; the Zemax Zebase library~\cite{zemax_opticstudio}, which predominantly features United States patents; and a public file exchange for lens designs~\cite{reiley_lens_design_exchange}, which contains community-submitted designs from diverse sources. Each collected design retains its corresponding sequence-file artifact and the best available source attribution, including a patent identifier, a textbook or journal citation, or another available source record.

Because the source artifacts use heterogeneous file formats, each design is converted to CodeV's digital lens format, represented by a \texttt{.seq} file, and corrected for residual source- or conversion-related issues. Many source files also contain generic glass codes or obsolete material labels rather than materials from available optical catalogs. We therefore use the CodeV \texttt{GLASSFIT} procedure to update the material assignments and then re-optimize the image-plane location to account for those material changes. These preparation steps and the subsequent evaluation runs are automated through CodeV scripts executed on the LLIMAS compute system~\cite{Doyle2017LLIMAS}.

Each prepared optical system is retained in two linked forms: the standardized sequence-file artifact and a tabular prescription row. Within the tabular prescription, the optical system is represented as a sequence of surfaces with associated geometric and material parameters. Figure~\ref{fig:lens_param} illustrates the lens assembly and the parameters used to define this representation. For each active surface $j$, the table records:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Radius of curvature $R_j$}: signed radius of curvature of surface $j$;
    \item \textbf{Thickness $t_j$}: axial distance between surfaces $j$ and $j+1$;
    \item \textbf{Material $j$}: optical material immediately following surface $j$;
    \item \textbf{Semi-diameter $h_j$}: radial aperture height of surface $j$;
    \item \textbf{Aperture stop index}: index of the surface designated as the aperture stop.
\end{enumerate}

Beyond the surface-level prescription, each optical system is associated with system-level conditions and sampling definitions that characterize its intended use:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First-order optical specifications}: effective focal length (EFL), entrance pupil diameter (EPD), f-number, object distance, and back focal length;
    \item \textbf{Packaging and image-plane quantities}: lens-to-image distance, overall length, total lens-track length, and minimum lens separation;
    \item \textbf{Field definition and sampling}: indexed x- and y-field coordinates together with the available angular, object-space, image-space, and ray-intercept coordinate representations;
    \item \textbf{Spectral sampling}: indexed wavelengths and their corresponding relative weights;
    \item \textbf{Structural descriptors}: aspheric-surface, element, and cemented-lens counts together with the aperture-stop designation, including whether the stop lies within the lens assembly.
\end{enumerate}

The collected corpus retains the source-specific values available for these quantities. The common field and wavelength conventions used to form the canonical subset are introduced in Section~\ref{sec:dataset_patent_shortlisting}.

The tabular prescription also retains optical-performance and sensitivity annotations generated during CodeV evaluation. The annotation families used in this study include:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Spot-size metrics}: field-indexed SPO67 and SPO100 measures;
    \item \textbf{Encircled-energy metrics}: field-indexed EE50 and EE90 measures;
    \item \textbf{Nominal performance}: root-mean-square (RMS) wavefront-error measures evaluated across the available field positions;
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity metrics}: field-indexed performance and tolerance sensitivities together with nominal alignment sensitivity.
\end{enumerate}
Unless otherwise specified, distances are reported in millimeters, angles in degrees, and wavelengths in nanometers. Additional column-level schema and distribution details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:dataset_details}.

Together, the linked source attribution, standardized sequence-file artifact, tabular prescription, operating conditions, and optical-performance annotations define the collected reference corpus. The following subsection describes the eligibility and standardization criteria used to derive the canonical patent subset for synthetic expansion and generative modeling.

\subsection{Patent Dataset Shortlisting}
\label{sec:dataset_patent_shortlisting}

To formulate a standardized starting point for generative modeling and synthetic dataset generation, we select and process designs from the collected reference corpus according to the following standardization and filtering criteria:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Designs are specified for object distance of infinity.
    \item Materials are selected from existing optical material catalogues.
    \item Object and image spaces are air.
    \item Object and image surfaces are represented as flat/planar surfaces without curvature.
    \item Aspheric surfaces and gratings are not considered.
    \item The number of surfaces ranges from 3 to 17.
    \item A maximum field angle is used as a design specification.
    \item Design performance is evaluated at $\{0, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0\}$ times the maximum field angle.
    \item Field angles are represented in the meridional/y-field direction, with x-field angle entries set to zero.
    \item Performance is evaluated in the visible spectrum using fixed wavelengths (F, d, C lines).
    \item The material between the last surface and image plane is air, and the penultimate material is glass.
\end{enumerate}

Applying these criteria gives a canonical subset of 1,253 confirmed patent designs selected from the broader 2,571-design patent-derived reference corpus. The retained rows keep the same tabular optical-prescription schema as the curated source corpus: non-applicable or structurally irrelevant cells are left empty, while fields affected by the standardization criteria are updated to the canonical form used for synthetic generation and modeling.\\

\subsection{Synthetic Expansion}
\label{sec:dataset_synthetic_expansion}

The synthetic dataset is generated from the canonical patent subset using perturbations that preserve the tabular prescription format described above. This expansion is intended to increase the number and coverage of available optical prescriptions while keeping each synthetic row traceable to a realistic patent-derived design family. At a high level, the synthetic construction process is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item sample a parent design from the canonical patent subset;
    \item perturb curvature, thickness, material, or combinations of these quantities;
    \item re-evaluate semi-diameters and image-plane placement using the optical evaluation pipeline;
    \item discard rows that fail the generation or simulation checks;
    \item retain successful rows with full lineage back to the parent design and perturbation source.
\end{enumerate}
Figure~\ref{fig:synth_data_pipe} summarizes this synthetic dataset construction workflow.

The resulting canonical dataset contains 1,126,333 rows: 1,253 patent-derived rows and 1,125,080 synthetic rows. The synthetic rows are retained in the same tabular prescription format as the patent-derived rows and are generated through curvature, thickness, material, and combined perturbation families. Provenance fields are retained so that synthetic rows can be traced back to their parent patent designs and perturbation sources.
